Comic Crime

Crime becomes plot fuel. The moral gravity of the genre is replaced by irony, chance, and the comedy of the failed plan. The question is not “who did it?” but “how does it go wrong?”

Definition

Comic Crime turns transgression into comedy. The story runs on plan, greed, and error. Justice may appear, but it arrives as accident, not as system.

Core tension: the individual vs the rule. Crime reveals social habits: money, status, fear, the theatre of morality.

Formula: crime makes us laugh when it exposes the ridicule of moral gravity — not its weight.

Branches

Branches of Comic Crime

Branches describe the crime form. They stay readable. They stay few.

These anchors are meant to be cited in guides.

Caper #caper

Plans, blunders, escalation. The plot is a chain reaction.

plan blunder escalation
Comic Noir #comic-noir

Dark humour, absurd violence, moral fog. Laughter is a survival reflex.

dark absurd violence irony
Heist / Burglar #heist

Craft meets chaos. A job requires method, the world provides friction.

craft method chaos
Humorous Cosy Mystery #cosy

A small world, a contained case. Comedy comes from ritual, gossip, and social roles.

small world ritual role-play
Campus Murder #campus-murder

Academic life meets crime. Status games turn into motive and method.

Bridge with Society: campus
status institution motive
Branch note #crime-form-note

When in doubt, pick the branch that best describes the plot machine: caper, heist, cosy, noir.

Tip: the same book can be Heist by form and Comic Noir by tone. Keep one as headline.
Narrative engines

Narrative engines (who drives the crime)

Engines are character motors. They are reusable. They keep reading simple.

Choose one main engine. Add a secondary engine only if it clarifies the plot.

The Schemer #schemer

A plan drives the story. The protagonist believes in control. The plot proves otherwise.

Subtypes (stable)
  • Mastermind — #schemer-mastermind
  • Small-time crook — #schemer-small-time
  • Con artist — #schemer-con-artist
The Hapless / Fumbling Criminal #hapless

Accident, naivety, bad luck. The character does not drive the crime. The crime drives the character.

Subtypes (stable)
  • Accidental criminal — #hapless-accidental
  • Bystander gone astray — #hapless-bystander
  • Naïve accomplice — #hapless-naive-accomplice
The Avenger #avenger

Revenge drives the plot. Moral legitimacy is unstable. Comedy comes from the gap between justice and method.

Subtypes (stable)
  • Wronged everyman — #avenger-wronged
  • Comic vigilante — #avenger-vigilante
  • Misguided redeemer — #avenger-misguided
The Survivor #survivor

Crime is a tool for staying afloat. Comedy comes from improvisation under pressure.

Subtypes (stable)
  • Desperate improviser — #survivor-desperate
  • Cornered dreamer — #survivor-cornered
  • Gruff outsider — #survivor-outsider
The Trickster / Buffoon #trickster

Chaos is the method. Crime is a pretext for misrule.

Subtypes (stable)
  • Slapstick gang — #trickster-gang
  • Serendipitous idiots — #trickster-idiots
  • Misrule agent — #trickster-misrule
The Comic Investigator #comic-investigator

Not the criminal, but the one who interferes. Comedy comes from method mismatch.

Subtypes (stable)
  • Professional bumbler — #investigator-bumbler
  • Amateur sleuth — #investigator-amateur
  • Reluctant investigator — #investigator-reluctant
Bridge rule: Engines can travel across currents. A “Schemer” can exist in Society, but in Crime the plan is a crime plan.
Crossovers

Crossovers (keep them as notes)

Comic Crime often borrows from Society. It also shares engines with Neo-Nonsense.

Keep crossovers as one-line notes. Do not stack labels.

Society ↔ Crime#crossover-society

Institution comedy can slide into crime when the plan appears. Campus is a common bridge.
