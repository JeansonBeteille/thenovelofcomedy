Currents

Three large dynamics organise the map of modern comic fiction: Comedy of Society, Crime Comedy and Neo-Nonsense. They are not genres. They are reading paths: a way of naming what comedy does to reality.

Orientation

What a “current” is (and is not)

A current is a broad reading axis: it groups novels that produce laughter through a similar friction between people, rules and reality. It does not freeze the work into a genre label: it helps us compare books without reducing them.

Current = “source” Primary layer

A current is the source family of comedy. Branches are the recognisable “models” inside it (Frustration, Schemer, Cosmic Bureaucracy…).

The map stays simple: each guide uses the same coordinates.

Then: zoom Same order everywhere
  • Branch — the dominant comic flavour within the current.
  • Environment — where bodies struggle: milieu, place, period.
  • Role — how comedy drives the novel: Engine, Thread, or Turn.

Labs are optional: they appear when several guides converge around a shared comic energy.

Current

Comedy of Society

Comedy of Society is the original comic in literary. The individual facing the collective: family, work, neighbourhood, institutions, hierarchies. Humour arises from the friction between the self and the rule.

Definition Reading axis

Comedy of Society stages the tension between belonging and resistance: the self confronted with codes, roles, institutions and the small mechanisms of social life.

Claim: Society comedy observes how people and rules constantly miss each other.

Main branches Zoom
  • Frustration
  • Winner / Comeback
  • Code / Etiquette
  • Vaudeville (form)
  • Edge
  • Campus
  • …
Close to Generic terms

social comedy, social satire, comedy of manners, domestic comedy, workplace comedy, campus novel.

Use “Close to” as a translation bridge: it connects TNoC vocabulary to library/catalogue terms.

Typical environments Where it plays
  • Schools & universities (technical college, campus)
  • Family & domestic interiors
  • Offices, administrations, small hierarchies
  • Neighbourhoods, clubs, micro-societies
Entry points Start here

If you like: brilliant losers, institutions that malfunction, codes that crush people — start with Society and pick a branch.

Current

Crime Comedy

The individual facing rule, law and morality. Crime, fault and transgression become comic material — often less to celebrate crime than to expose how moral systems bend, crack, or look ridiculous.

Definition Reading axis

Comedy comes from failed plans, impossible compromises, flexible moralities, and plots where chance ends up replacing justice.

Claim: Crime becomes funny when it reveals the ridiculousness of moral systems more than the gravity of acts.

Main branches Zoom
  • Schemer
  • Hapless Criminal
  • Avenger
  • Survivor
  • Trickster
  • Comic Investigator
  • Murder Campus
  • …
Close to Generic terms

comic noir, caper novel, heist comedy, farcical thriller, satirical crime fiction, black comedy.

A bridge for readers: “crime fiction” vocabulary, but with comedy as the driving lens.

Typical environments Where it plays
  • Backrooms of cities (bars, cheap hotels, crews)
  • Institutions (police, courts, bureaucracies)
  • Suburbs, small towns, local moralities
  • Campuses when crime meets codes
Entry points Start here

If you like: plans collapsing, criminals not quite competent enough, justice replaced by chaos — start with Crime and select a branch (Schemer, Hapless Criminal…).

Current

Neo-Nonsense

The individual facing the universe. Neo-Nonsense appears in a world where certainties crack: ideologies, grand theories, illusions of control — and where science, objects and systems become comic matter.

Definition Reading axis

It combines cinematic grammar, modern science, object-characters, existential self-mockery and fully conscious logical games.

Claim: The least extravagant logic in a world that no longer guarantees anything.

Main branches Zoom
  • Cosmic Bureaucracy — comic SF as paperwork, codes, protocols, cosmic “administration”.
  • Assistant Comedy — AI not as dystopia, but as servility, jealousy, meta-interaction.
  • Hypochondriac Metaphysics — the body as panic of meaning; symptoms as philosophy.
  • Canon Hijack — secondary character takeover; a canon rewritten from inside.
Close to Generic terms

absurdist fiction, comic SF, surreal comedy, metafiction, postmodern satire, philosophical comedy.

“Close to” is deliberately plural here: Neo-Nonsense is where criticism often splits labels.

Typical environments Where it plays
  • Systems and “machines” (bureaucracies, labs, tech stacks)
  • The universe as rulebook (science, metaphysics, cosmic logistics)
  • Anxious bodies (medical corridors, symptoms, self-monitoring)
  • Canon territories revisited (myths, classics, inherited stories)
Entry points Start here

If you like: science as comic material, object-characters, meta-narratives, logical games — start with Neo-Nonsense and pick a branch (Cosmic Bureaucracy, Canon Hijack…).

  • Example guide: James (Percival Everett)
  • Douglas Adams can anchor “Cosmic Bureaucracy” when the dedicated guides are online.
Method

How to use currents with the guides

Currents are the top-level compass. The guides are the heart. Use one to reach the other.

Reader path Simple
  • Pick a novel you love → open its guide.
  • Note its current and branch → click to explore siblings.
  • Compare environments → recognise recurring “stages”.
  • Compare roles (Engine/Thread/Turn) → understand the author’s comic intention.
Editor path Building the atlas

When a corpus grows, a Lab may appear naturally (a cluster of works sharing a similar comic energy). Until then, currents + branches + environments + roles are enough to keep the map sharp.

This is why the cartography never competes with the text: it supports it.
