Currents of modern comic fiction

Comedy is one of the most precise ways of reading reality.

— The Novel of Comedy

The Novel of Comedy proposes a new map of laughter in the novel. Transnational. Transhistorical. Transgeneric.

Not closed genres. Narrative engines. Ways of inhabiting the world.

The map serves the texts. It helps us see the mechanics of laughter, the singularity of style, and the context in which a line suddenly becomes precise. Sometimes heroic.

Current I

Comedy of Everyday

The individual facing the collective. Society. Codes. Institutions.

The world does not run smoothly. Everyone pretends it does. Laughter begins there.

Definition. Engine.

  • Definition. The chronicle of daily life. Couple. Work. Hierarchy. Group life. The original matrix of comedy.
  • Engine. A person tries to integrate, to flee, or to détourner society. Comedy measures the gap between the desire to belong and the social world as it actually is.
  • Key formula. Laughing at what holds people together. Laughing at what separates them.

Structural DNA

  • Social code. The rule, the club, the family, the institution shapes the comic.
  • Repetition. Ritual. The daily becomes mechanical. Laughter comes from the return of the same.
  • Linguistic friction. Politeness, jargon, false fronts. Language becomes arena.
  • Failure to adapt. The protagonist resists, fails, adjusts. Laughter grows there.
  • Light redemption. Comedy often reconciles without resolving.

Current II

Comedy of Crime

The individual facing the rule. Law. Morality. A forbidden act as comic terrain.

Definition. Engine.

  • Definition. The tragedy of wrongdoing inverted into moral farce. The crime becomes a mirror. The novel looks for the logic of failure, not the purity of justice.
  • Engine. Transgression. The forbidden act opens a space of freedom, satire, and organized disorder. Laughter rises from greed, calculation, and absurd plans.
  • Key formula. Crime becomes funny when it reveals the ridiculousness of moral systems, not the gravity of acts.

Narrative DNA

  • Joyful transgression. Crime as invention.
  • Plan-failure comedy. Failure as structure.
  • Revealing crime. A mirror of social flaws.
  • Mechanical burlesque. Vaudeville applied to wrongdoing.
  • Moral irony. Justice replaced by chance and irony.

Current III

Neo-Nonsense

The individual facing the universe. A post-war current. Arts and science enter the comic line.

In a block universe where past, present and future spin on the same axis, Neo-Nonsense may be the least extravagant logic.

Definition. Engine.

  • Definition. Neo-Nonsense emerges from the artistic and scientific turbulence after World War II. It is not a return to Victorian nonsense. It is a modern mutation.
  • Engine. A person tries to grasp meaning in a world where explanatory systems are fragile. Humour becomes direction. Resistance. Understanding. Extraction.
  • Key formula. The least extravagant logic.

Five DNA axes

  • Cinematic grammar. Cuts, ellipses, oral rhythm. The novel becomes an inner camera.
  • Science as comic material. Physics, biology, medicine, technology. Not décor. Material.
  • Object-character. Things act, decide, insist. Matter participates.
  • Existential self-mockery. The “I” as comic lab. Body, symptoms, obsessions.
  • Playful paradox. Logical games, conscious absurdities. Nonsense as method.
