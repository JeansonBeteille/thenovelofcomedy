Definition

What is a “current”?

A current is a large, readable family of comic energies. It is not a rigid genre label. It helps you navigate quickly, while keeping the guides comparable.

In practice: each novel guide is placed in one current, and optionally refined by a branch. The toolbox adds a separate indicator called Comedy centrality (Comic Engine / Thread / Turn).

Comedy of Society is the original current of social comedy, centered on everyday life and institutions. In the 20th century, Crime Comedy turns crime into a comic terrain. After World War II, Neo-Nonsense integrates scientific and existential vertigo. Society (integration / refusal) · Crime (transgression) · Neo-Nonsense (vertigo of meaning).

Current

Comedy of Society

Institutions collide with private life: satire, manners, social pressure.

Typical scenes: work hierarchies, families, schools, clubs, class codes, polite cruelty. Comedy often comes from norms and the cost of violating them.

Core idea

Institutions (visible or invisible) produce friction. People behave badly, often with style.

institutions status manners
Typical branches
  • Satire (systems, class, ideology)
  • Manners (codes, romance, embarrassment)
  • Campus (academia, rituals, status games)

Current

Crime Comedy

Capers, schemes, elastic morality: crime as a comic device.

The plot is often a machine: plans, mistakes, improvisations, consequences. Comedy comes from competence gaps, moral shortcuts, and criminal rationality.

Core idea

Plot mechanics become comedic: heists, capers, cover-ups, misdirection.

schemes escalation moral elasticity
Typical branches
  • Caper (plans, blunders, escalation)
  • Comic noir (dark edges, absurd violence)
  • Heist / burglar (craft, chaos)

  • Tourist Season
  • Florida Roadkill
  • Burglar · Mondrian
Current

Neo-Nonsense

Absurdist and postmodern comedy: rigorous logic in a world that slips.

The surface may be calm, the sentences controlled, but the world follows a skewed logic. Comedy emerges from glitches, systems, and tonal shifts.

Core idea

Rigour meets crack. The comic effect can be structural or tonal.

absurd logic postmodern structure
Typical branches
  • Absurd (deadpan chaos, logic puzzles)
  • Postmodern satire (media, systems, paranoia)
  • Meta / Formal (narration as device)
