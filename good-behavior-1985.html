1 · Reading note

Reading note

▶

Sirens, alarms, rooftops: Dortmunder starts this sixth caper already running. After slipping into a Manhattan convent and striking a deal with nuns (yes, nuns), he assembles a crew wobblier than ever. Goal: the 71st floor of an absurdly secure tower. When we say “heading for the top”, we mean it literally.

Westlake’s 1980s stride is Olympian: dry, sharp, and never quite as unreal as you’d like it to be. Under the farce: the uncomfortable question — what becomes of morality when it meets convenience, and when power looks like ownership plus security guards.

5 · Context

Context of writing

▶

Mid-1980s New York: money as ideology, towers as temples, security as theology. Westlake pushes Dortmunder into a world where “law” is often just private infrastructure.

That’s the key context: a heist novel where the main opponent isn’t police work — it’s the ecosystem of ownership, procedures, and reputation.

6 · Style

Style & writing

▶
A · Sentence mechanics

Westlake’s humour is dry and procedural: the prose stays calm while the world becomes impossible. He piles up practical details — then lets the plan collapse under its own “reason”.

B · Rhythm

Escalation by overload: each fix adds variables; each variable is a future accident. The comedy remains legible because the narration never “winks”: it simply records.
