1 · Reading note

Reading note

The wail of sirens and alarms opens this sixth Dortmunder caper at the top. And up high Dortmunder will go. After slipping through a Manhattan convent and striking a deal with nuns (yes, nuns), he assembles a team wobblier than ever. The goal: the 71st floor of the most secure tower on Fifth Avenue. When we say Dortmunder is heading for the top, we mean it.

Westlake’s writing hits an Olympian stride in the 1980s: sharp, unhinged and not so unrealistic. One regret, though: with a last name starting with W, I'm always forced to dig at the back of the bookstore shelves for a Donald novel. My poor knees...

5 · From the desk

From the desk

A · Place in the work

Good Behavior belongs to a phase where Westlake brings more explicit geopolitical and moral stakes into Dortmunder under the cover of farce. Here, the question is not “to steal or not to steal”, but what becomes of morality when it meets convenience — and when power becomes indistinguishable from ownership.

B · Mechanics

Westlake proceeds by rule and overload: each system believes itself perfect; he brings them into contact and creates the spark. Silent convent, hyper-secure tower, asset ideology, New York logistics — guaranteed chaos.

C · In the sentence

Westlake piles up detail and reflection with an agitated rhythm that matches the twists and turns. Example (short excerpt): “He had many reasons to be depressed … Nuns, for instance.”

6 · Reception

Reception

  • Critics:
    – Washington Post Book World: Jean M. White emphasized that Westlake “never allows his comedy to slide into … buffoonery.”
    – The New York Times Book Review (June 22, 1986), Newgate Callendar, in the “Crime” column: he praises this Dortmunder and stresses that Westlake can “make a comment on society” and that the book has “a strong cutting edge.”
  • Within the series: often read as a peak of the heist / satire / burlesque triptych.
