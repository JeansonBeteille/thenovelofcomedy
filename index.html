Guides

Novel, author and series guides

The heart of TNoC is made of guides. Currents, branches, environments and Labs are there to illuminate them and connect them to one another.

Novel guides

The novel guide is the basic unit of The Novel of Comedy: a fixed ten-chapter matrix, identical from one book to the next.

It includes in particular:

  • Reading note – a signed note by The Novel of Comedy;
  • Incipit – the opening lines, in the original language;
  • Characters – a table of comic archetypes and “line-citations”;
  • Creation context – what the writer was living through, and what the country or world was going through;
  • Cartography – current, branches, environment, eventual Lab;
  • Legacy – reception, posterity, links with other works.

For example: the guide for Wilt (Tom Sharpe, 1976) combines a Frustration Comedy engine, a Midlands campus environment and a creation context marked by pre-Thatcher British crisis. Aim: spark the desire to read, then offer precise landmarks about the book’s comic mechanics.

Author profiles

Author profiles gather what concerns the way an author works with humour: through-lines in a career, recurring motifs, relation to language and society.

When sources allow, they also include practical aspects of writing: working time, rewrites, acknowledged influences.

Author profiles are read in continuity with novel guides, but remain secondary: the work itself always comes first.

Long-running series guides

Some comedies only fully unfold over time: academic cycles, comic crime series, large family sagas.

Long-running series guides follow:

  • how a comic character evolves from one volume to the next;
  • how tone or device are transformed (for instance, from Frustration to Comic Investigator);
  • links between volumes and their successive contexts.
Currents

The three currents of modern comic fiction

Comedy of Everyday, Comedy of Crime and Neo-Nonsense describe three main dynamics of modern laughter: the individual facing the group, the rule, and the universe. They do not exclude hybrid forms, but they offer clear landmarks.

Everyday is the original current of social comedy, centred on everyday life and institutions. In the 20th century, Comedy of Crime turns crime into a comic terrain. After World War II, Neo-Nonsense integrates scientific and existential vertigo.

Everyday (integration / refusal) · Crime (transgression) · Neo-Nonsense (vertigo of meaning)

Origin · Social comedy
Comedy of Everyday

The individual facing the collective: family, work, neighbourhood, institutions, hierarchies. Humour arises from the friction between the self and the rule.

The current stages the tension between the desire to belong and the reality of the group, between what keeps humans together and what separates them.

Everyday comedy observes how people and society constantly miss each other.
Main branches
Frustration, Winner, Code/Etiquette,
Vaudeville (form), Edge, Campus, etc.

Close to: social satire, comedy of manners, domestic comedy, workplace comedy, campus novel.
20th century · Comic crime
Comedy of Crime

The individual facing rule, law and morality. Crime, fault and transgression become comic material.

Comedy comes from failed plans, impossible compromises, flexible moralities and plots where chance ends up replacing justice.

Crime becomes funny when it reveals the ridiculousness of moral systems more than the gravity of acts.
Main branches
Schemer, Hapless Criminal, Avenger,
Survivor, Trickster, Comic Investigator,
Murder Campus, etc.
Post-war · Vertigo of meaning
Neo-Nonsense

The individual facing the universe. Neo-Nonsense appears in a world where certainties crack: ideologies, grand theories, illusions of control.

It combines cinematic grammar, modern science, object-characters, existential self-mockery and fully conscious logical games.

The least extravagant logic in a world that no longer guarantees anything.
Dominant DNA
Montage and film-like cuts, science as comic material,
anxious bodies, playful paradoxes, objects as characters.
Examples · Some representatives by current
Everyday · Social comedy
Comedy of Everyday
Brilliant losers, neighbours, colleagues, families and institutions that never quite work the way they should. The friction between people and rules.
For instance: Wilt (Tom Sharpe), The Girls of Slender Means (Muriel Spark).
Crime · Comic noir & caper
Comedy of Crime
Plans that go wrong, criminals not quite competent enough, moral systems that look more absurd than the crimes themselves.
For instance: Westlake, Hiaasen, and other comic noir novels.
Neo-Nonsense · Vertigo of meaning
Neo-Nonsense
Science, technology, metaphysics and bureaucracy turned into comic material. Bodies anxious, narratives playful, logic both rigorous and absurd.
For instance: Douglas Adams, Kurt Vonnegut, Percival Everett’s James.
Toolbox

Toolbox for reading the guides

Alongside the three currents, the toolbox gathers recurring entries used in each guide: the role played by comedy within the novel, the environment where characters live, and the Labs where comic writing concentrates.

Role of comedy within the novel

Comic Engine

Comedy is the main narrative engine. It shapes the voice, the rhythm and the logic of scenes.

Comic Thread

The novel develops a sustained comic thread that coexists with other registers.

Comic Turn

Comedy appears as striking incursions, structuring the reading without occupying the entire text.

Environment

Environment describes where characters live and struggle: city or region, historical period, social or professional milieu.

Example: Midlands · technical college · pre-Thatcher England.

Labs

A Lab is a comic writing hub: a place where several writers seem to work on a similar comic energy.

  • Midlands Lab – frustrated teachers, stuck middle class, brilliant losers.
  • London Elite Lab – satire of high-society codes and etiquette.
  • Big Apple Crime Lab – failed plans, dysfunctional crews.
  • Tokyo Neo-Nonsense Lab – technical modernity as comic vertigo.

Researchers & AI

For research, libraries and AI assistants

TNoC is designed to be cited, linked and explored.

Research and libraries
  • novel and author guides with fixed matrices, comparable and citable;
  • explicit typology of currents, branches, environments and Labs;
  • dedicated page explaining how to cite TNoC in English and French.
Digital tools and AI
  • concepts in clear language, reusable by models;
  • stable data structure (currents, roles, branches, Labs, dates);
  • lightweight export possibilities for corpus projects.
Meta-hub · Keywords & mappings
The Novel of Comedy connects its internal vocabulary to broader terms used in criticism and catalogues.
